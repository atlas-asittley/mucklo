# Suspension Setup Basics for Track Days

Getting your suspension dialed is the single most impactful thing you can do for lap times. More than power. More than tires. Here's how to get started.

## Understanding the Fundamentals

Your suspension does one thing: keep the tires in contact with the track. Everything else — spring rates, damping, alignment — is in service of that goal.

### The Four Key Adjustments

1. **Ride Height** — How high or low the car sits
2. **Spring Rate** — How stiff the springs are
3. **Damping** — How quickly the springs compress and rebound
4. **Alignment** — Camber, caster, and toe angles

## Ride Height

Lower is generally better for track use, within reason.

- **Lower center of gravity** = less body roll = more grip
- **Too low** = insufficient suspension travel = bottoming out = bad

A good starting point is to lower the car 1-1.5 inches from stock, then fine-tune from there.

### Corner Balancing

Ideally, you want equal weight on diagonally opposite wheels (LF+RR = RF+LR). This is called corner balancing and requires adjustable coilovers and a set of scales.

## Spring Rates

Stiffer springs reduce body roll and keep the car more stable, but there's a point of diminishing returns.

### Choosing Spring Rates

- **Street/track dual duty** — Moderate rates (6-8K front, 4-6K rear for most cars)
- **Track only** — Stiffer rates (8-12K front, 6-10K rear)
- **Time attack** — Very stiff (12K+ front, 10K+ rear)

These are general guidelines. The right rate depends on your car's weight, weight distribution, and intended use.

## Damping

Damping controls how quickly the springs move. Think of it as the "speed control" for your suspension.

### Compression vs Rebound

- **Compression** — Resistance when the spring compresses (hitting a bump)
- **Rebound** — Resistance when the spring extends (after a bump)

### Setting Damping

1. Start at the middle of the adjustment range
2. Drive the car and feel how it reacts
3. If the car bounces after bumps → more rebound
4. If the car feels harsh over bumps → less compression
5. Make small adjustments (2-3 clicks at a time)

## Alignment

Track alignment is very different from street alignment.

### Camber

Negative camber (top of the tire tilted inward) is essential for cornering.

- **Street** — -0.5° to -1.0°
- **Track** — -2.0° to -3.5°
- **Time attack** — -3.0° to -4.5°

More negative camber = more cornering grip, but less straight-line braking grip.

### Caster

More positive caster = more straight-line stability and better steering feel. Most cars benefit from as much positive caster as possible.

### Toe

- **Front toe** — Slight toe-in (0.1°) for stability, or zero toe for turn-in response
- **Rear toe** — Slight toe-in (0.1-0.2°) for stability under power

## Putting It All Together

A good setup follows this process:

1. **Set ride height** — Start with equal height at all four corners
2. **Choose spring rates** — Based on your intended use
3. **Set damping** — Start in the middle, adjust by feel
4. **Set alignment** — Start with proven specs for your platform
5. **Test and iterate** — Drive, feel, adjust, repeat
