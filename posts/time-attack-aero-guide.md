# The Complete Guide to Time Attack Aerodynamics

Aerodynamics is the most misunderstood area of race car development. In time attack, where single lap times determine everything, getting your aero package right can be worth seconds per lap — more than any engine mod.

## The Basics

At its core, aerodynamics on a race car is about two competing forces:

- **Downforce** — pushes the car into the track, increasing grip
- **Drag** — resists forward motion, reducing top speed

The goal is to maximize downforce while minimizing drag. In practice, it's a trade-off, and the right balance depends on your car, track, and class rules.

## Front Splitter

The front splitter is often the first aero modification and one of the most effective.

### How It Works

A splitter creates a pressure differential. Air above the splitter is at higher pressure (pushing down), while air below is accelerated and at lower pressure (pulling down). The result is net downforce on the front axle.

### Key Design Factors

- **Rake angle** — The angle of the splitter relative to the ground. More rake = more downforce, but also more drag.
- **End plates** — Prevent high-pressure air from spilling around the sides. Critical for maintaining the pressure differential.
- **Proximity to ground** — Closer to the ground = more effective. But you need enough clearance for track surfaces and suspension travel.

## Rear Wing

The rear wing works like an airplane wing, but upside down. Instead of generating lift, it generates downforce.

### Airfoil Selection

The shape of the wing cross-section determines its efficiency. Key parameters:

- **Camber** — More camber = more downforce, but also more drag
- **Thickness** — Thicker airfoils are more forgiving but less efficient
- **Aspect ratio** — Higher aspect ratio (taller, narrower) = more efficient, but packaging constraints often limit this

### Mounting

Wing location matters more than most people think:

- **Height** — Higher mounting gets the wing into cleaner air, above the turbulent boundary layer
- **Distance from rear** — Further back = more leverage = more effective
- **Angle of attack** — More angle = more downforce, until it stalls

## Diffuser

The underbody diffuser is the secret weapon of aerodynamic efficiency.

### The Venturi Effect

A diffuser works by expanding the air volume under the car. As the air expands, it accelerates, creating a low-pressure zone that literally sucks the car to the ground.

### Why Diffusers Are So Efficient

Unlike wings and splitters, diffusers generate downforce with very little drag penalty. This makes them the most efficient aerodynamic device on a race car.

### Flat Floor

For a diffuser to work, you need a flat floor. The flat floor acts as the "top" of the venturi tunnel. Without it, the diffuser can't create the pressure differential it needs.

## Aero Balance

Getting the front-to-rear balance right is crucial.

- **Too much front aero** = understeer at speed (front has more grip than rear)
- **Too much rear aero** = oversteer at speed (rear has more grip than front)
- **Balanced aero** = neutral handling, maximum cornering potential

Most time attack cars aim for slightly more rear downforce than front, as this provides stability under braking and on corner exit.

## Testing and Validation

Theory is great, but nothing beats real-world testing:

1. **Start with baseline** — Run the car with no aero to establish a reference
2. **Add one element at a time** — Understand the effect of each component
3. **Use data** — GPS, accelerometer, and lap timing data tell you what's actually happening
4. **Iterate** — Small adjustments, test, repeat

## Common Mistakes

- **Adding aero without suspension upgrades** — More downforce = more load on springs and dampers
- **Ignoring the underside** — The bottom of the car matters as much as the top
- **Copying without understanding** — What works on one car may not work on yours
- **Too much too soon** — Big wings and splitters on stock suspension is a recipe for disappointment

## Conclusion

Aerodynamic development is a journey, not a destination. Start simple, understand each element, and build your package over time. The results will come.
